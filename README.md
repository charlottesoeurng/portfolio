# Charlotte's Portfolio

Personal website built with Vue 3 and Vite, deployed on GitHub Pages.

## Tech Stack

- Vue 3 (Composition API)
- Vite
- CSS custom properties

## Structure

- `src/components/` — About, Skills, Projects, Resume, NavBar
- `src/assets/` — Per-component stylesheets
- `public/` — Static assets (images, resume PDF)

## Development

```bash
npm install
npm run dev
```

## Deployment

```bash
npm run build
```

## Reflection

I love Technology, Art, and Philosophy. I am drawn to things that allow me to apply my problem solving skills to help others around me while still being able to utilize my creativity. The topics above don’t appear to be related but after four years of taking countless classes and meeting countless people at the University of St. Thomas, I have come to understand how things that seem completely different from each other can be connected.

There is however a problem that bothers me: does better technology actually better society and humankind? On top of all the nice things that technology gives us, everything we create can serve as tools to harm, deceive, and exploit people just as easily if used the wrong way.

As a soon-to-be computer science graduate,  I have learned to think about my problems in terms of steps and systems such as what the big picture is, what needs to happen, how to get there, what the edge cases are that I need to address, etc. By my definition, software is good enough when it is reliable, maintainable, and solves real problems that currently or will exist. But Computer Science alone does not tell you which problems are actually worth solving, or who gets left out when a product is built for one audience and not another.

But as someone who is deeply interested in philosophy and abstract concepts, I see technology and problem solving through a second lens. I have learned to question things that have no clean answer, such as what the underlying assumption is when I’m developing my products, or who my audience is and what their perspective is. I have learned to distinguish between a good enough software product and a software product that actually improves the lives of the people it touches. I realized that my users are not just a concept but real people with real needs, expectations, and differences, and I may not be able to understand them through my computer screen and lines of codes.

Computer Science and Philosophy shaped my way of thinking in a way that I will always be mindful of products I build and that I will ensure what I build does more good than harm, if any at all.

The best example of this is the Minnesota Alliance on Crime website, my senior capstone project that I built as seen on my portfolio. MAC is a nonprofit organization that supports crime victims by providing assistance in different ways such as domestic violence support, legal representation, and services for victims of general crime. Our team built a resource directory website that survivors and community members could actually use to find help.

As the lead frontend developer and UI designer of the project, I was responsible for designing the look, feel, and usability of the site across different devices and browsers. The technical work involved building Vue.js components, structuring navigation for different screen sizes, and ensuring the interface was clean and accessible to every user with different needs and backgrounds. But the most important decisions to make were not technical. They were questions like: What does someone in crisis actually need to see when they land on this page? How do we make this directory as simple as possible for someone with limited tech knowledge? How do ensure these resources feel trustworthy to the public? What information should we show and what detail do we hide?

My interest in philosophy allowed me to deeply think of answers to those questions rather than just deciding on what is easiest for me to do and what looks best on the screen, while considering client feedback and requirements. This project resulted in client satisfaction as not only did we build a crime resource directory site, but we also implemented something that connects to users on a personal level through our interface.
This experience confirmed to me that a good software product is rather ethical and impactful to the common good.

However, I cannot say that all of my projects were as successful as MAC. Cadenci (named after “Candence”) was a fun music recommendation web application I started building in the fall of 2025 that focuses on helping people find new music based on song opening lines. As an avid music listener who is always looking for new songs for my playlist, I was extremely proud of this idea as I knew there were people like me who love searching for new songs and forming opinions on those new songs as soon as the opening line and beat start. I finished building the front end template, integrating the design system, and even loaded initial songs into the database before I eventually had to give up due to legal constraints I hadn’t considered.

I learned that licensing music, especially well known ones, for my own software is an extremely expensive and difficult process that I could not physically and financially afford. Displaying song lyrics without valid authorization from artists or their labels is a serious copyright violation, and I was not going to risk those problems for a small hobby project. I should not have started building before I understood all legal implications and consequences of the product I was excited to make. The project failed and I had to set it aside.

Since then, I have learned to start doing important research before creating all my products as I do not want to stall development due to prior lack of information again.

My growth coming out of the University of St. Thomas is not only technical, but it is also ethical, philosophical, and cultural. In my AMCD course, I wrote a research paper on the overlooked culture of front doors. I explored how their designs signal a sense of belonging, status, personality, and identity across American culture. This paper changed how I approach the art of designing my sites.

A front door is like a web interface. It tells visitors who is welcome and who is not, what the values inside are, and what kind of place it is. Every design decision communicates something to the user about how they belong on the site they choose to interact with.


After graduation, I want to work in web development, building real products that help real people in need. I wish to apply everything I studied to benefit the common good of society.
